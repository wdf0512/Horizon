## [Building Durable Workflows Using Only PostgreSQL](https://www.dbos.dev/blog/postgres-is-all-you-need-for-durable-execution) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A new article from DBOS details a practical architectural pattern for implementing durable workflow execution directly on PostgreSQL, without relying on external workflow engines. This approach simplifies infrastructure and reduces operational overhead for reliable workflows, as many applications already use PostgreSQL, making durable execution more accessible and challenging the need for dedicated workflow systems. The pattern leverages database transactions for atomic step execution; however, it may lack built-in retry policies, advanced visibility, debugging tools, and other features that mature workflow engines like Temporal provide out of the box.

hackernews · KraftyOne · May 28, 18:41 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48313530)

**Background**: Durable execution guarantees that long-running processes survive failures and resume from the last completed step. Workflow engines like Temporal manage state via their own event histories, but PostgreSQL’s ACID transactions can serve as a reliable state store, turning the database into the source of truth. DBOS (formerly Database-Oriented Operating System) is an open-source library originating from MIT and Stanford research that wraps this pattern, effectively embedding workflow logic within database transactions.

## [Various LLM Smells: Identifying AI-Generated Writing Patterns](https://shvbsle.in/various-llm-smells/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A curated list identifies specific phrases (e.g., 'honest caveat:', 'the smoking gun:') and structural patterns (e.g., contrastive negation) that characteristically appear in LLM-generated text, while community members discuss leveraging LLMs for writing critique rather than direct output to preserve personal style. As LLM-generated content floods the internet, recognizing these 'smells' helps preserve authenticity, empowers readers and writers to detect AI-written material, and encourages more thoughtful integration of LLMs as editorial aids rather than replacements for human voice. Notable patterns include introductory tags like 'The honest answer:' followed by a colon, overuse of terms like 'load bearing' (non-architectural) and 'blast radius' (non-explosive), and the rhetorical device of contrastive negation ('not just X, but Y'). The patterns stem from LLMs' predictive text nature, leading to repetitive, formulaic structures and vocabulary that become recognizable across many outputs.

hackernews · speckx · May 28, 19:02 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48313810)

**Background**: The term 'smells' borrows from software engineering's 'code smells', referring to surface indicators that may point to deeper problems. Large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT generate text by predicting likely word sequences based on training data, resulting in stylistic tendencies that differ from human variation. Awareness of these 'AI writing smells' has grown among writers, editors, and online communities as a way to discern machine-generated content.
